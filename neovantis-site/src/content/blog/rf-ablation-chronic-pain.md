## Understanding Chronic Musculoskeletal Pain

Chronic pain — defined as pain persisting beyond the normal tissue healing period, typically three months or longer — represents one of the most prevalent and economically significant health conditions globally. In the musculoskeletal domain, conditions such as facet joint syndrome, sacroiliac joint dysfunction, and degenerative disc disease account for a substantial proportion of chronic back and neck pain presentations in specialist pain management clinics.

The management of these conditions has historically been dominated by pharmacological approaches, including non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and opioid analgesics. While these agents provide short-term symptomatic relief for some patients, they carry significant side-effect profiles and do not address the underlying neural pain mechanisms. Radiofrequency (RF) ablation has emerged as a compelling interventional alternative that targets pain at source — with a safety profile and durability that few pharmacological options can match.

## What Is Radiofrequency Ablation?

Radiofrequency ablation is a minimally invasive procedure in which a specialised electrode, placed under fluoroscopic or ultrasound guidance, delivers a controlled radiofrequency current to a target nerve or neural structure. The resulting thermal energy — typically targeting tissue temperatures of 60–90°C — creates a controlled lesion that interrupts the transmission of pain signals along that nerve pathway.

The procedure is typically performed under light sedation as a day case, with patients returning home within a few hours. Recovery times are short, and the vast majority of patients tolerate the procedure well with minimal post-procedural discomfort.

## Mechanism of Action

At the target nerve, the RF current generates an ionic oscillation that produces resistive heating in the immediately adjacent tissue. When the tissue temperature at the electrode tip reaches the therapeutic threshold, axonal conduction is disrupted — effectively denervating the pain-generating structure.

Two primary RF modalities are used in clinical practice:

**Conventional (Continuous) RF Ablation.** Delivers continuous current to produce a sustained thermal lesion. This modality is used when the target nerve can be safely lesioned — most commonly for the medial branch nerves innervating lumbar and cervical facet joints.

**Pulsed RF.** Delivers brief pulses of high-voltage RF energy with cooling intervals between pulses, avoiding sustained thermal lesion formation. This is used near mixed sensory-motor nerves where thermal injury must be avoided, such as in dorsal root ganglion applications and certain peripheral nerve procedures.

## Clinical Indications

The evidence base for RF ablation is strongest in several well-defined clinical scenarios:

### Lumbar Facet Joint Denervation
The most widely performed RF procedure, targeting the medial branch nerves supplying the lumbar zygapophysial (facet) joints. Patients with confirmed facet-mediated low back pain — diagnosed by a positive response to medial branch nerve blocks — are the ideal candidates. Published evidence supports significant pain reduction and improved function lasting 6–18 months or longer in appropriately selected patients.

### Cervical Facet Denervation
Analogous to the lumbar procedure, cervical medial branch RF ablation addresses facet-mediated neck pain and cervicogenic headache. Particularly relevant in post-whiplash populations and patients with chronic cervical spondylosis.

### Sacroiliac Joint Pain
The sacroiliac (SI) joint is an increasingly recognised source of lower back pain, accounting for up to 25% of chronic low back pain presentations. Lateral branch RF ablation targeting the SI joint innervation provides clinically meaningful relief in well-selected patients, with results comparable to lumbar medial branch procedures.

### Knee Genicular Nerve Ablation
Targeting the superior medial, superior lateral, and inferomedial genicular nerves supplying the knee joint capsule, this procedure is gaining momentum as a non-surgical option for patients with chronic knee osteoarthritis who are not yet candidates for or wish to defer arthroplasty.

## Patient Selection

Appropriate patient selection is the most critical determinant of RF ablation outcomes. The standard pathway requires:

1. Confirmed clinical diagnosis consistent with a neural pain generator amenable to RF treatment
2. Positive response to diagnostic nerve block(s) with local anaesthetic — typically defined as ≥50–80% pain reduction
3. Failure or inadequate response to conservative management including physiotherapy and pharmacological treatment
4. No contraindications including local infection, coagulopathy, or implanted electronic devices in proximity

## The RF Procedure: Equipment Requirements

Successful RF ablation requires purpose-built equipment combining precision energy delivery with real-time temperature monitoring:

**RF Generator.** The generator delivers controlled radiofrequency current at 500kHz, with continuous temperature feedback from the electrode tip thermocouple. Modern generators support both conventional and pulsed RF modes from a single platform, with digital controls and procedure logging.

**RF Electrodes.** Thermocouple electrodes are available in a range of gauges (18–22G), lengths (50–150mm), and active tip lengths (5–20mm). Active tip length determines the size of the thermal lesion — longer active tips are used when targeting larger nerve structures or when anatomical accuracy is less certain.

**Grounding Pad.** A dispersive electrode (grounding pad) applied to the patient's thigh or back completes the electrical circuit and ensures safe dispersal of the return current.

## Duration of Effect and Retreatment

One of the clinically significant advantages of RF ablation is its durability. Because the procedure physically disrupts the target nerve, relief is not dependent on ongoing drug levels or patient compliance. The majority of patients with a positive response experience meaningful pain relief for 6–24 months.

Over time, the ablated nerve may regenerate — and with it, pain may return. In most cases, a repeat ablation procedure produces equivalent or superior results to the index procedure, making RF ablation a sustainable long-term management strategy rather than a one-time intervention.

## Conclusion

Radiofrequency ablation represents a mature, evidence-supported interventional technique that addresses a significant gap in the chronic pain management armamentarium. By targeting pain at the neural level with a minimally invasive approach, RF ablation offers durable relief with a favourable safety profile — reducing the burden of long-term pharmacotherapy and improving patient quality of life.
