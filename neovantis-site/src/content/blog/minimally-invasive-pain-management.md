## The Burden of Chronic Pain
<div class="page-hero-bg" style="background-image:url('/images/neovantis-clinton-visagie.png')"></div>
Chronic pain is estimated to affect one in five adults globally, representing an extraordinary burden of human suffering and economic productivity loss. In South Africa, as in high-income countries, the treatment of chronic musculoskeletal pain — back pain, neck pain, joint pain, and neuropathic conditions — consumes a substantial and growing proportion of healthcare resources.

For decades, the dominant treatment paradigm for chronic pain was pharmacological: escalating doses of analgesics, anti-inflammatories, and opioid medications, often with diminishing returns and significant adverse effect profiles. The global opioid crisis has catalysed a profound reassessment of this approach, accelerating interest in interventional pain management techniques that target pain pathways directly rather than masking them systemically.

Minimally invasive interventional techniques now occupy a central position in the chronic pain management pathway — offering targeted, durable relief with limited systemic exposure, short recovery times, and evidence-based outcomes for appropriately selected patients.

## The Spectrum of Interventional Pain Management

Interventional pain management encompasses a range of procedures that vary in invasiveness, target anatomy, mechanism of action, and durability of effect. Understanding this spectrum is essential for appropriate patient triage and treatment planning.

### Diagnostic and Therapeutic Nerve Blocks

Nerve blocks using local anaesthetic agents serve a dual function: they confirm the anatomical diagnosis by producing short-term pain relief, and they provide temporary therapeutic benefit. Common applications include:

- **Medial branch nerve blocks** for facet joint pain confirmation
- **Selective nerve root blocks** for radicular pain diagnosis
- **Sacroiliac joint injections** for SI joint pain
- **Trigger point injections** for myofascial pain syndromes

While the relief from a single nerve block is typically transient — lasting hours to days — repeated blocks can provide cumulative benefit and serve as the diagnostic gateway to more durable interventions such as RF ablation.

### Radiofrequency Ablation

RF ablation represents the most established and evidence-supported interventional technique for durable pain relief in the context of facet joint, sacroiliac joint, and genicular nerve pain. By creating a controlled thermal lesion in the target nerve, RF ablation provides pain relief that outlasts any pharmacological or block-based approach — typically 6–24 months from a single procedure, with the option to repeat as needed.

The procedure is performed under fluoroscopic guidance as a day case, with a typical procedure time of 30–60 minutes. Modern RF generators support multiple ablation modalities — conventional, pulsed, and cooled RF — from a single platform, giving the proceduralist flexibility to address a range of clinical scenarios.

### Epidural Steroid Injections

Epidural steroid injections (ESIs) deliver corticosteroid and local anaesthetic into the epidural space adjacent to an inflamed nerve root or disc herniation. They are most effective in the management of acute radicular pain from disc herniation or foraminal stenosis — providing a window of pain relief that allows patients to engage with rehabilitative physiotherapy.

ESIs are not a long-term solution; repeated injections carry systemic steroid risks and their efficacy diminishes with repeated use. They are best understood as a bridging strategy within a broader multimodal treatment plan.

### Intrathecal Drug Delivery

For patients with refractory chronic pain inadequately managed by conventional means, intrathecal drug delivery systems (implanted infusion pumps delivering opioids or other agents directly into the cerebrospinal fluid) represent a highly specialised option. By delivering analgesia at a fraction of the systemic dose, intrathecal systems provide superior pain control with markedly reduced systemic opioid burden.

This is a subspecialist intervention requiring careful patient selection, multidisciplinary input, and experienced implanting surgeons.

### Spinal Cord Stimulation

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) uses implanted epidural electrodes to deliver electrical current to the dorsal columns of the spinal cord, modulating pain signal processing through well-characterised neurophysiological mechanisms. SCS has strong evidence for neuropathic pain conditions including failed back surgery syndrome, complex regional pain syndrome, and diabetic neuropathy.

## Image Guidance: The Foundation of Safe Interventional Practice

A unifying feature of modern interventional pain procedures is the use of image guidance — most commonly fluoroscopy or ultrasound — to ensure accurate needle and electrode placement. Image guidance is not merely a convenience; it is a patient safety imperative.

Fluoroscopy allows real-time visualisation of bony landmarks and needle trajectory in the context of spinal anatomy — critical for epidural, facet, and SI joint procedures where proximity to neural structures demands precision.

Ultrasound guidance has transformed peripheral nerve block technique, enabling real-time visualisation of soft tissue anatomy, nerve structures, and needle advancement in procedures targeting the hip, knee, shoulder, and peripheral nerves.

## Patient Selection and the Multidisciplinary Model

The most effective interventional pain management is delivered within a multidisciplinary framework that integrates specialist physician assessment, physiotherapy, psychological support, and pharmacological management. Interventional procedures should be understood as one component of a comprehensive treatment plan — not a standalone solution.

Careful patient selection is the strongest predictor of procedure success. Key principles include:

- **Confirmed anatomical diagnosis** consistent with the proposed neural target
- **Documented failure** of conservative management (typically 6–12 weeks of physiotherapy and appropriate pharmacotherapy)
- **Positive response to diagnostic blocks** before proceeding to ablative procedures
- **Realistic patient expectations** regarding the nature and expected duration of benefit
- **Exclusion of contraindications** including local infection, coagulopathy, and psychological red flags

## The Role of Equipment Quality

Across all interventional pain procedures, the quality and reliability of equipment is a non-negotiable determinant of clinical outcomes. RF generators that deliver inaccurate temperature readings, electrodes that fail to maintain target temperature, or grounding pads that produce inconsistent current dispersal all compromise the reliability of the procedure.

For proceduralists, access to equipment from trusted, reputable manufacturers — backed by clinical support and technical expertise — is essential to delivering consistent, safe outcomes for their patients.

## Conclusion

Minimally invasive interventional pain management has matured from a niche subspecialty into a central pillar of comprehensive chronic pain care. The convergence of image guidance technology, purpose-built procedure equipment, and an expanding evidence base has made these techniques safer, more accessible, and more effective than at any previous point in their history.

As awareness of the limitations — and harms — of long-term opioid therapy continues to grow, interventional approaches will play an increasingly prominent role in South African pain management practice.
